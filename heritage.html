The Heritage Village

An Amerindian Heritage Complex project proposal.

Heritage Complex Proposal Key Elements

AN AMERINDIAN HERITAGE COMPLEX

A Vision for Cultural & Ecological Preservation (First Proposed in 1998)


This proposal is designed to cover a number of issues of vital concern to the members of the Santa Rosa First Peoples Community, advanced with the intent of preserving and developing the Amerindian cultural heritage of Trinidad and Tobago. By creating a dedicated land-base and model village, we aim to make a significant contribution to both the ecological patrimony and the cultural tourism sectors of our nation.

Key Elements of the Proposal

1. Introduction & Global Rationale

Across the globe, there is a growing recognition that Indigenous Peoples require a land base to ensure their cultural survival. Whether in Australia, Canada (Nunavut), or our Caribbean neighbors Dominica and Guyana, possessing designated land is a standard, essential requirement for preserving ancestral connection and ecological stewardship.

2. Ecological Stewardship

The Heritage Village is conceived as a sanctuary for the environment. By practicing traditional, sustainable methods of land use, the project aims to counter ecological threats such as deforestation, soil erosion, and forest fires, showcasing a model of harmonious co-existence with nature.

3. Eco-Tourism & Cultural Tourism

Trinidad and Tobago's tourist potential can be expanded through eco-tourism. Following the successful models in Dominica and St. Vincent, a model Amerindian Village would serve as an educational and cultural attraction, generating sustainable livelihoods and foreign exchange for the community and country.

4. Safeguarding Retained Traditions

Our community maintains a rich, living heritage that we wish to share and pass down in this complex:

  • Cassava Culture: Growing cassava and processing it into traditional cassava bread, farine, and cassareep.
  • Traditional Building: Constructing homes using indigenous materials like tapia (mud walling) and timite palm thatch.
  • Weaving: Crafting utensils and mats using terite reed and mamu vines.
  • Medicinal Knowledge: Preserving ancient knowledge of forest plants and herbs for healing.
  • Sacred Rituals: Conducting spiritual smoke ceremonies alongside historical festivals.
